# CHAPTER 1

## THE PROBLEM AND ITS BACKGROUND

### Introduction

Schools handle massive amounts of student data every day. enrollment forms, grades, attendance records, and teacher assignments all need tracking. At Kiwalan National High School (KNHS), much of this still runs on paper and manual processes. Teachers spend hours filing documents. Administrators search through cabinets to find a single record. When someone needs a transcript or a report, the process can take days.

This is not unique to KNHS. Schools across the Philippines face similar challenges. But the scale of the problem varies. KNHS serves a large student population with limited administrative staff. The school needs a system that can keep up with growing demands without burning out its people.

The Portal for Records, Information, and School Management (PRISM) proposes a web-based solution. It aims to centralize records, speed up information access, and reduce the workload on school staff. This study looks at how PRISM was developed and whether it actually solves the problems it claims to address.

### Background of the Study

Kiwalan National High School is a public secondary school in Kiwalan, Iligan City. It serves students from the surrounding barangays and has grown steadily over the past decade. With more students come more records. The school currently manages enrollment data, grades, attendance, and faculty information through paper-based systems and basic spreadsheets.

These methods worked when the school was smaller. Now they create bottlenecks. Retrieving a student's record might mean digging through folders in a storage room. Generating a report for the Division Office requires manually compiling data from multiple sources. Teachers who should spend time teaching end up doing clerical work instead.

Several schools in the region have adopted digital systems. DepEd has its own platforms like LIS (Learner Information System) and the Electronic School Form 5. But these are national systems with specific purposes. They do not address the day-to-day internal management needs of a single school. KNHS needs something built for its own workflow.

Web-based systems offer a practical answer. They do not require installing software on every computer. Staff can access them from any device with internet. Updates happen in one place and propagate everywhere. For a school with limited IT resources, this matters.

### Statement of the Problem

This study aims to design, develop, and evaluate a web-based system called PRISM for Kiwalan National High School. Specifically, it seeks to answer the following questions:

1. What are the current challenges in record management and information access at KNHS?
2. What features should PRISM include to address these challenges?
3. How should PRISM be designed using the ADDIE model?
4. How well does PRISM perform in terms of functionality and usability based on user evaluation?

### Research Objectives

**General Objective:**

To design, develop, and evaluate a web-based Portal for Records, Information, and School Management (PRISM) for Kiwalan National High School.

**Specific Objectives:**

1. Identify the current problems in manual record-keeping and information retrieval at KNHS.
2. Determine the functional and technical requirements for PRISM.
3. Develop the system following the ADDIE instructional design model.
4. Evaluate the system based on user satisfaction, functionality, and usability.

### Significance of the Study

This study benefits the following groups:

**Students.** PRISM gives them faster access to their own records. They can check grades, enrollment status, and other information without waiting in line at the registrar.

**Teachers.** The system reduces the time spent on administrative tasks. Teachers can encode grades, check attendance, and generate reports through the portal instead of filling out forms manually.

**Administrators.** PRISM centralizes data management. Administrators can pull reports, track enrollment trends, and monitor school operations from a single dashboard.

**DepEd Division Office.** The system can generate standardized reports that match the formats required by the division, reducing errors and delays in submission.

**Future Researchers.** This study provides a reference for similar projects. It documents the development process, challenges encountered, and solutions applied.

### Scope and Delimitations

This study covers the design and development of a web-based system for Kiwalan National High School. It focuses on student records management, grade encoding, attendance tracking, and report generation.

The system is designed for use by students, teachers, and administrators of KNHS. It does not integrate with DepEd's LIS or other national platforms. The study does not cover hardware procurement or internet infrastructure improvements.

User evaluation was limited to respondents from KNHS. Results may not generalize to other schools with different contexts.

### Conceptual Framework

This study uses the ADDIE model as its conceptual framework. ADDIE stands for Analysis, Design, Development, Implementation, and Evaluation. It is a systematic approach commonly used in instructional design and systems development.

The framework guides the entire process of building PRISM:

**Analysis.** This phase identifies the problems at KNHS. It examines the current workflows, gathers requirements from users, and defines what the system needs to do. The goal is to understand the gap between the current situation and the desired outcome.

**Design.** Based on the analysis, this phase creates the system architecture. It includes database design, interface layouts, user flow diagrams, and security considerations. The design phase produces blueprints that guide development.

**Development.** This is where the system gets built. Developers write code, create databases, and assemble the components defined in the design phase. Testing happens alongside development to catch issues early.

**Implementation.** The system gets deployed and introduced to users. Training sessions help students, teachers, and administrators learn how to use PRISM. Feedback from this phase often leads to adjustments.

**Evaluation.** Users assess the system based on predefined criteria. This includes functionality, usability, and satisfaction. The evaluation determines whether PRISM meets its objectives and identifies areas for improvement.

### Theoretical Framework

This study draws on several theories that support the use of information systems in education.

**Technology Acceptance Model (TAM).** TAM explains how users adopt new technology. It identifies two key factors: perceived usefulness and perceived ease of use. If users believe PRISM will help them and is easy to use, they are more likely to adopt it. This theory guided the evaluation criteria for the system.

**Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology (UTAUT).** UTAUT extends TAM by adding performance expectancy, effort expectancy, social influence, and facilitating conditions. These factors help explain why some users accept PRISM while others resist it.

**Information Systems Success Model (DeLone and McLean).** This model identifies six dimensions of information system success: system quality, information quality, service quality, use, user satisfaction, and net benefits. It provided the framework for evaluating PRISM's effectiveness.

These theories grounded the study's approach to both development and evaluation.

### Definition of Terms

**Web-Based System.** A system that runs on a web browser and requires internet access. Users interact with it through a web interface rather than installing software locally.

**Records Management.** The process of creating, maintaining, storing, and disposing of records. In schools, this includes student files, grades, attendance, and administrative documents.

**PRISM.** Portal for Records, Information, and School Management. The web-based system developed in this study for KNHS.

**ADDIE Model.** A framework for instructional design consisting of five phases: Analysis, Design, Development, Implementation, and Evaluation.

**Usability.** The degree to which a system can be used effectively, efficiently, and with satisfaction by specified users.

**User Satisfaction.** The degree to which users are content with a system and its outputs.

**Functional Requirements.** The specific behaviors or functions a system must perform.

**Non-Functional Requirements.** Characteristics such as performance, security, and scalability that define how a system operates rather than what it does.
