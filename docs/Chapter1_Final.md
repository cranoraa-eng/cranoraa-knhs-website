# CHAPTER 1

## THE PROBLEM AND ITS BACKGROUND

### Introduction

Schools process large volumes of student data daily. enrollment forms, grades, attendance sheets, and teacher assignments all need tracking. At Kiwalan National High School (KNHS), much of this still runs on paper. Teachers file documents by hand. Administrators search through cabinets for a single record. Getting a transcript or report can take days.

This is not unique to KNHS. Schools across the Philippines face similar issues. But the scale varies. KNHS serves a growing student population with limited staff. The school needs a system that can handle increasing demands without overloading its people.

PRISM (Portal for Records, Information, and School Management) proposes a web-based solution. It aims to centralize records, speed up information access, and reduce the administrative burden. This study examines how PRISM was built and whether it works.

### Background of the Study

Kiwalan National High School is a public secondary school in Kiwalan, Iligan City. It serves students from nearby barangays and has expanded over the past decade. More students means more records. The school currently manages enrollment data, grades, attendance, and faculty information through paper-based systems and spreadsheets.

These methods worked when the school was smaller. Now they create delays. Retrieving a student record might require digging through folders in a storage room. Generating a report for the Division Office means manually compiling data from different sources. Teachers who should be teaching end up doing clerical work.

Some schools in the region have gone digital. DepEd has platforms like LIS (Learner Information System) and the Electronic School Form 5. These are national systems with specific purposes. They do not handle the internal day-to-day management needs of a single school. KNHS needs something built for its own workflow.

Web-based systems offer one way forward. They run in a browser, so no software installation is needed on every computer. Staff can access them from any device with internet. Updates happen once and apply everywhere. For a school with limited IT support, this matters.

### Statement of the Problem

This study aims to design, develop, and evaluate a web-based system called PRISM for Kiwalan National High School. Specifically, it seeks to answer:

1. What are the current challenges in record management and information access at KNHS?
2. What features should PRISM include to address these challenges?
3. How should PRISM be designed using the ADDIE model?
4. How well does PRISM perform in terms of functionality and usability based on user evaluation?

### Research Objectives

**General Objective:**

To design, develop, and evaluate a web-based Portal for Records, Information, and School Management (PRISM) for Kiwalan National High School.

**Specific Objectives:**

1. Identify the current problems in manual record-keeping and information retrieval at KNHS.
2. Determine the functional and technical requirements for PRISM.
3. Develop the system following the ADDIE instructional design model.
4. Evaluate the system based on user satisfaction, functionality, and usability.

### Significance of the Study

This study benefits the following groups:

**Students.** PRISM allows them to access their records faster. They can check grades, enrollment status, and other information without waiting in line.

**Teachers.** The system cuts down time spent on administrative tasks. Teachers can encode grades, check attendance, and generate reports through the portal instead of filling out forms by hand.

**Administrators.** PRISM centralizes data management. Administrators can pull reports, track enrollment, and monitor school operations from one dashboard.

**DepEd Division Office.** The system generates standardized reports in formats required by the division, reducing errors and submission delays.

**Future Researchers.** This study provides a reference for similar projects. It documents the development process, challenges, and solutions.

### Scope and Delimitations

This study covers the design and development of a web-based system for Kiwalan National High School. It focuses on student records management, grade encoding, attendance tracking, and report generation.

The system is designed for students, teachers, and administrators of KNHS. It does not integrate with DepEd's LIS or other national platforms. The study does not cover hardware procurement or internet infrastructure improvements.

User evaluation was limited to respondents from KNHS. Results may not apply to other schools with different contexts.

### Conceptual Framework

This study uses the ADDIE model as its conceptual framework. ADDIE stands for Analysis, Design, Development, Implementation, and Evaluation. It is a systematic approach commonly used in instructional design and systems development.

The framework guides the entire process of building PRISM:

**Analysis.** This phase identifies the problems at KNHS. It examines current workflows, gathers requirements from users, and defines what the system needs to do. The goal is to understand the gap between what exists and what is needed.

**Design.** Based on the analysis, this phase creates the system architecture. It includes database design, interface layouts, user flow diagrams, and security considerations. The design phase produces blueprints for development.

**Development.** This is where the system gets built. Developers write code, create databases, and assemble the components defined in the design. Testing happens alongside development to catch issues early.

**Implementation.** The system gets deployed and introduced to users. Training sessions help students, teachers, and administrators learn how to use PRISM. Feedback from this phase often leads to adjustments.

**Evaluation.** Users assess the system based on predefined criteria. This includes functionality, usability, and satisfaction. The evaluation determines whether PRISM meets its objectives and what needs improvement.

### Theoretical Framework

This study draws on several theories that support the use of information systems in education.

**Technology Acceptance Model (TAM).** TAM explains how users adopt new technology. It identifies two key factors: perceived usefulness and perceived ease of use. If users believe PRISM will help them and is easy to use, they are more likely to adopt it. This theory guided the evaluation criteria.

**Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology (UTAUT).** UTAUT extends TAM by adding performance expectancy, effort expectancy, social influence, and facilitating conditions. These factors help explain why some users accept PRISM while others resist it.

**Information Systems Success Model (DeLone and McLean).** This model identifies six dimensions of success: system quality, information quality, service quality, use, user satisfaction, and net benefits. It provided the framework for evaluating PRISM's effectiveness.

These theories grounded the study's approach to development and evaluation.

### Definition of Terms

**Web-Based System.** A system that runs on a web browser and requires internet access. Users interact with it through a web interface rather than installing software locally.

**Records Management.** The process of creating, maintaining, storing, and disposing of records. In schools, this includes student files, grades, attendance, and administrative documents.

**PRISM.** Portal for Records, Information, and School Management. The web-based system developed in this study for KNHS.

**ADDIE Model.** A framework for instructional design consisting of five phases: Analysis, Design, Development, Implementation, and Evaluation.

**Usability.** The degree to which a system can be used effectively, efficiently, and with satisfaction by specified users.

**User Satisfaction.** The degree to which users are content with a system and its outputs.

**Functional Requirements.** The specific behaviors or functions a system must perform.

**Non-Functional Requirements.** Characteristics such as performance, security, and scalability that define how a system operates rather than what it does.
